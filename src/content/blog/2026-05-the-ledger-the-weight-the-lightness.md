title: "The Ledger, The Weight, The Lightness"

<pre class="poem">
I was the whole world.
Not a partner – a foundation.
The thing she stood on
while she learned to stand.
Sobriety built on my back.
Every morning I was there.
Every crisis, every slip, every crawl back
from the edge of herself –
I was the hand she reached for.
She relied on me for everything.
Not just comfort, not just presence –
for how to think.
For what was good and what was bad.
For where the edges of right and wrong were.
I was her compass, her floor, her voice
when she couldn’t find her own.

But a compass has no one to point it home.
A floor has no one to stand on.
And somewhere in all of it
I had stopped taking up space.
I built her a house out of second chances,
furnished each room with the benefit of doubt,
swept the same wreckage from the same floors
until my hands forgot what rest felt like.

She tried, in the ways she could.
A meal on the table, her body in the bed –
those she gave freely, wanted even.
But the rest of her was always elsewhere.

But a mouth on my skin
and a good meal on the table
is not the same as safety.
Is not the same as being known.
I told her this.
Tried to find the words
that would open a door in her.

She heard it as complaint.
Or didn’t hear it at all.

I had opinions. Ones I’d thought through.
But I held them loosely –
always willing to be moved
by a good argument,
by honest exchange,
by someone willing to meet me
in the middle of an idea.

She mistook that for a threat.
When I drew a line around myself
she couldn’t read it as protection.

Heard it instead as instruction –
as if my limits were a lesson
I was trying to teach her,
a shape I wanted her to take.

As if I needed her to agree with me
in order to be okay myself.

I wasn’t asking her to think like me.
I was asking for the room
to think at all.
So I softened the lines.
Made excuses I didn’t quite believe.
Told myself the story
that kept me standing next to her –
because I couldn’t leave someone unsafe.
That’s just who I am.
The kind of person who stays
past the point of sense
because the alternative
was imagining her falling
with no one there to catch her.

The resentment would collect quietly,
weeks of it, wordless,
and then arrive all at once
like something that had been building pressure
behind a wall neither of us could see.

No repair after.
No sitting down in the wreckage together.
Just the explosion,
then the running,
then the silence,
then back to cooking and pleasing
as if that had ever been the question.

She stopped asking what else she could give.
Decided the answer was already in her hands.
And I kept trying to explain
that what I needed
couldn’t be cooked or performed –
just offered.

Just said out loud.
Just once.

She was a storm I kept calling weather.
A pattern I kept calling a phase.

I didn’t leave to wound her.
I left because years of reaching
had worn my arms down to nothing
and I needed to remember
I had arms at all.

She ran.
That was her answer to hard things.
And I kept following the distance,
kept trying to close the gap,
kept choosing the relationship
she kept leaving on the floor.

And then I stayed anyway.
Even after I ended it.
Of course I did.
Kept showing up.
Kept holding the weight.

Someone found me in that quiet.
Reminded me what warmth felt like –
not just in the body
but in the actual exchange of two people
facing each other without flinching.

I didn’t tell her.
That part is mine to carry.
But she’d had years to say sorry.
Years to turn toward me.
Years to notice the person
standing in her weather
getting soaked.

She never did.
Instead after that out it came –
everything she’d been saving,
sharp and ready,
like she’d been waiting
for permission to be done with me.

That’s not hurt.
That’s inventory.
And I was standing there
still wanting to apologize
to someone who never once
thought to apologize to me.

That’s when I understood:
she was never lost.
She knew exactly where she was.
She just needed someone who didn’t.

She told me once I made her rape about me.
That I had taken her pain and centered myself.
But I was the one who sat with her in it.
Who named what happened when she couldn’t.
Who persuaded her that what was done to her
was not okay,
was not her fault,
was not something she had to carry quietly.
I gave her the words for her own wound.
And years later she sharpened them
and handed them back.

And I carried it too.
Not the same – I know that –
but I sat inside the aftermath with her,
felt the edges of what had been done to her
press themselves into my sleep,
my body,
the way I moved through ordinary days.

Second hand trauma has no clean name.
No one asks how I am
when I am the one who held the person
who was broken.
She never asked.
Never thought to.

Then the years of watching over her anyway.
Because I wanted her well
and that part was always true.
I celebrated every step forward
like it was my own –
maybe because by then
it was hard to tell the difference.
And then she left.
Not taken. Not lost.
Just ready.
Finally ready.
Walking out on her own legs
into her own life.
I watched her go
and felt something I wasn’t prepared for.

Not grief.
The door closed
and the quiet came in
and I stood in it
like a man who had forgotten
what his own silence sounded like.

I didn’t know how heavy it was
until I put it down.
All those years I called generosity –
the vigils, the weather, the wreckage,
the gap I kept trying to close –
I felt them leave my body
like a breath I had been holding
since before I knew I was holding it.
I loved her enough to see her through.
That was real.

But God –
the lightness.

The strangest part is this:
I spent years looking for safety in her
and never found it.

Not once.

Always one wrong word away
from watching her disappear.

Now she is gone
and I am safe.
Not safe from her.
Safe in myself.
The ground under my feet
finally still.

And I am still paying.
Not to her – that debt is settled.
But in the way I hesitate
before I am simply myself with a woman.
In the way I monitor my own presence,
second-guess what was never questionable,
watch a normal moment
from slightly outside it.
She never knew she left that in me.
Never thought to ask.
The trauma I carried for her
found somewhere to live
after she was gone.
</pre>
